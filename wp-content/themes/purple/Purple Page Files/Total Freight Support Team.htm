International Shipments

 

At the current moment, you can only process INTL shipments for LTL on FedEx Freight My Account. The only thing you are required to do is add Broker Information. If they don’t have a broker, they can call Freight Tech Support to set up a Broker contract.

 

With the release of CAFÉ 2600 and Freight on FedEx Ship Manager, you will be able to create commercial invoices for freight. This means several changes in the handling of LTL INTL.

 

1)      We will help customers set up and print out Commercial Invoices

2)      LTL Freight customers will have to file for an EEI using AES Direct, not FedEx. However, they will have to fill out the EEI information on the shipment once complete.

3)      They will still have to obtain a broker.

4)      As this is freight, there are no Dangerous Goods. And since it is INTL, there are no Hazardous Materials. If they need to ship dangerous goods, they will have to ship Express Freight.

 

General Freight Information

 

How do I find out about Freight Surcharges? Great question. There’s a document called the FedEx Freight 100 Series Rules Tariff. It contains all of the additional surcharges that might apply to a shipment. You can view that here: http://www.fedex.com/us/freight/rulestariff/tableofcontents.html

 

How do I get accurate rate quotes? Glad you asked. FedEx has created a great tool for getting rate quotes. You can find that here: Freight Central: LTL Freight Classification Tool-FedEx

 

What is an NMFC Code? / What do I do if my customer’s NMFC number is rejected by CAFÉ, Freight My Account, or FedEx Ship Manager Freight?  For most products, Freight costs are calculated by Class, which is a measurement of Density. Thus, the greater the density is at a given weight, the cheaper it is. Space is at a premium, weight is not. However, some products have their own NMFC class code defined by the National Motor Freight Transit Authority. They have a really expensive book that outlines all of these codes. However, some of our solutions don’t accept those codes. In those cases, just use the FedEx LTL Freight Classification Tool above. It will have you select the item you are shipping, and then it will ask for weight and dimensions. The class will take into account the NMFC code of your product. If your product doesn’t appear, it will give you a toll free number to get the class.

 

Being familiar with surcharges and how freight rate quotes are calculated are essential to providing customer service and instilling confidence in the customer.

 

Account Executive Support

 

We don’t require our Account Executives to be product experts. That’s what we’re here for. However, there are times when a lack of product expertise can cause issues that you need to be prepared to handle. This mostly happens when expectations are set that we can’t actually meet, due to the AE’s misunderstanding of the issue.

 

To understand how these issues arise, we need to first understand what Freight is and what Freight isn’t.

 

Freight is shipping pallets or boxes on a 53’ tractor trailer. They have a DOT maximum weight of 80,000 pounds. The truck itself ways a little more than 35k, so there is roughly 40k pounds free for shipping packages and pallets. Because weight is not an issue in this case, space is the limiting factor to maximizing profit. As such, the NMFC Class, from 50-500, represents the density of a product. The lower the class, the higher the density, and the cheaper the freight is, given the same weight. This is substantially different than what happens on a Ground or Express shipment. The primary factor for Ground and Express is weight, with dimensions being a secondary factor.

 

Because of that, people can misunderstand what shipments would be best suited for Freight, and what would be best suited for ground. Generally speaking, heavy shipments weighing more than 150 pounds should go Freight, shipments weighing 150 pounds or less should go ground. This is mostly because ground shipments aren’t very dense in compared to LTL shipments. Thus, they would be drastically more expensive to ship LTL than ground.

 

You can ship a 25 pound package freight, but it will cost you ten times as much.

 

People who are new to freight might not understand these differences. You can use the eFreight NMFC Class Finder or the FedEx Class Finder Tool combined with the FedEx Desktop Customer Tools to demonstrate the differences between the prices when shipping ground verses shipping LTL.

 

 

LTL Hazardous Materials

 

This is probably the least supported aspect of FedEx LTL Freight. These customers are not supported by the LTL Freight Tech Support, nor are they supported by the Dangerous Goods group. These customers don’t have to be flagged in ANY FedEx system as HazMat Certified, nor does FedEx provide any HazMat certification to these customers. They have to obtain their certifications separate from FedEx. They also have to fill out a majority of their documentation without any support from FedEx.

 

The majority of the Hazardous Materials regulations appear in a document called HMR, or Hazardous Materials Regulations. These are defined as CFR 49 parts 100 to 185. CFR refers to the Code of Federal Regulations. Title 49 specifically deals with transportation.

 

The two most important documents in a Freight agent’s arsenals are: How to Use Hazardous Materials Regulations and The Electronic Code of Federal Regulations Title 49. These two documents contain the sum of all government regulation on Freight shipping in general, and Hazardous Materials more specifically.

 

Frequently asked HazMat questions include:

 

  • How do I process Hazmat on Freight? First check the “HM” box when processing the shipment. Make sure the Technical Name and UN Number is in the description. Choose a HazMat package type, as its different than regular freight package types. Make sure the HM Offeror Name and Vendor Code is in one box, and the phone number is in the other box. Both are for the person or group responsible for 24 hour emergency support of Hazardous Materials for the customer. The vendor code is for companies that use a Third Party Hazmat Emergency Response company’s service, identifying their account.
  • Can I ship a pallet with some Hazmat materials of the same UN number and non-hazmat materials on the same pallet? The answer is Yes.
  • Can I ship a pallet with two Hazmat materials of different UN numbers on the same pallet? Per T. Glenn Foster, Chief of the Regulatory Review and Reinvention Branch, Standards and Rulemaking Division, Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration, a branch of the Department of Transportation, “Hazardous materials may be packaged together with other hazardous and non-hazardous materials as prescribed in §§ 173.24(e)(4) and  173.21(e) of the HMR” (link)
    • Section 173.24(e)(4):

(4) Mixed contents. Hazardous materials may not be packed or mixed together in the same outer packaging with other hazardous or nonhazardous materials if such materials are capable of reacting dangerously with each other and causing—

(i) Combustion or dangerous evolution of heat;

(ii) Evolution of flammable, poisonous, or asphyxiant gases; or

(iii) Formation of unstable or corrosive materials.

    • Section 173.21(e) FORBIDDEN:

 (e) A material in the same packaging, freight container, or overpack with another material, the mixing of which is likely to cause a dangerous evolution of heat, or flammable or poisonous gases or vapors, or to produce corrosive materials.

  • How can I ship multiple items including hazmat or multiple hazmat items on a single palette?
    • Under the description field, list the items separated by commas. If you were shipping engine parts and diesel, you would put “engine parts, diesel” followed by the UN number. If you are shipping multiple items, you would put in the technical name and un number, followed by a comma, and then the second technical name and un number.
  • How do I determine if my mixed HazMat pallet will violate Section 173.24(e)(4) or Section 17.321(e) by reacting dangerously with each other causing combustion or dangerous evolution of heat, evolution of flammable, poisonous, or asphyxiant gasses, or formation of unstable or corrosive materials? You can either call the company that provides your emergency 24 hour Hazardous Materials Support, or contact a chemist. These interactions are complicated enough that nothing short of an expert opinion will suffice.
  • Should I bundle my Hazardous Materials on my pallet, or should I ship them separately? Your Hazardous Materials and the items on your pallet will likely have different classes. If you put your Hazardous Materials on your pallet, you will have to calculate the class of the entire pallet and not rely on the class of an individual item. Depending on whether adding the hazardous material changes the dimensions and weight enough to change the base class, you might determine it is cheaper to ship them separately. However, you cannot determine this until you determine the class of the entire pallet based solely on weight and dimensions, and compare that with your main shipment’s individual class.
  • How do I process a mixed shipment of Hazardous Materials and non-Hazardous Materials on the same pallet? You would put the description of the non-hazardous items, separated by commas, and then for the hazardous materials you would put in the technical name and UN number, also separated by commas. Make sure you check the HM box and choose a HM package type.
